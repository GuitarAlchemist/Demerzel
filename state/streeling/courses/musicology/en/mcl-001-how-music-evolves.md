# How Music Evolves — A Brief History of Western Music

> **Department of Musicology** | Level: Beginner | Duration: 30 minutes

## Objectives

After this lesson, you will be able to:
- Name the six major periods of Western music and their approximate dates
- Identify how harmony and instrumentation changed across periods
- Recognize at least one key composer per era
- Trace the guitar's journey from the Renaissance to the modern stage

---

## 1. Why Music Changes

Music does not evolve in a vacuum. Wars, technology, religion, trade routes, and sheer human restlessness push it forward. Each period in Western music history was a reaction to what came before — sometimes a gentle evolution, sometimes a full-on rebellion.

Understanding these shifts is not about memorizing dates. It is about hearing *why* a Baroque fugue sounds nothing like a Romantic symphony, and why both sound nothing like a medieval chant.

---

## 2. Medieval Period (c. 500–1400)

**What it sounded like:** Monophonic chant — a single melodic line, no harmony. Later, early polyphony emerged: two or three voices moving in parallel.

**Key developments:**
- Gregorian chant — the foundation of Western melody
- The invention of musical notation (Guido d'Arezzo, c. 1000) — music could finally be written down and transmitted
- Early polyphony (organum) — the radical idea that two notes could sound at the same time

**Key figure:** Hildegard of Bingen — composer, mystic, and one of the first named composers in history.

**The guitar:** Did not exist yet. Its ancestors — the oud (Arabic world) and the vihuela (Spain) — were already being played. The oud arrived in Europe via Moorish Spain, planting the seed.

### Listening Checkpoint

If you listen to Gregorian chant and then to a Pérotin organum (c. 1200), you can hear the leap from one voice to many. That leap took centuries.

---

## 3. Renaissance (c. 1400–1600)

**What it sounded like:** Rich vocal polyphony — multiple independent voices weaving together in complex harmony. Music became more expressive and human-centered.

**Key developments:**
- Full polyphony — four, five, six voices moving independently
- The rise of secular music alongside sacred music
- Printed music (after 1501) — suddenly anyone with money could buy sheet music
- Consonance and dissonance began to be codified

**Key figures:** Josquin des Prez (mastery of vocal polyphony), Giovanni Palestrina (the ideal of smooth, balanced counterpoint).

**The guitar:** The four-course Renaissance guitar and the vihuela became popular in Spain and Italy. Lute music flourished — much of it is played on classical guitar today.

---

## 4. Baroque Period (c. 1600–1750)

**What it sounded like:** Ornate, dramatic, layered. The bass line became a structural foundation (basso continuo). Music developed a sense of key and harmonic direction.

**Key developments:**
- Tonal harmony — the major/minor key system that still dominates popular music
- Opera was invented (Monteverdi, c. 1607)
- Instrumental music became as important as vocal music
- Counterpoint reached its peak (fugues, canons)
- The harpsichord and organ were king

**Key figures:** Johann Sebastian Bach (the supreme contrapuntalist), Antonio Vivaldi (concerto master), George Frideric Handel (opera and oratorio).

**The guitar:** The five-course Baroque guitar was popular for accompaniment. The lute began its decline. Bach wrote lute suites that guitarists still perform — some of the most important pieces in the guitar repertoire.

---

## 5. Classical Period (c. 1750–1820)

**What it sounded like:** Clean, balanced, elegant. Less ornamentation than Baroque, more emphasis on melody supported by harmony. Structures became formalized — sonata form, symphony, string quartet.

**Key developments:**
- Sonata form — a template for musical storytelling (exposition, development, recapitulation)
- The symphony orchestra took its modern shape
- The piano replaced the harpsichord
- Music became public entertainment — concert halls, not just churches and courts

**Key figures:** Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart (melodic perfection), Joseph Haydn (father of the symphony and string quartet), Ludwig van Beethoven (bridged Classical and Romantic).

**The guitar:** The six-string guitar as we know it emerged around 1800. Fernando Sor and Mauro Giuliani wrote the first serious classical guitar repertoire — etudes, sonatas, concertos.

---

## 6. Romantic Period (c. 1820–1900)

**What it sounded like:** Big, emotional, personal. Orchestras doubled in size. Chromaticism pushed harmony to its limits. Composers became celebrities.

**Key developments:**
- Extreme emotional expression — music as autobiography
- Chromaticism — using notes outside the key for color and tension
- Nationalism — composers drew on folk music (Dvořák, Grieg, Chopin)
- The orchestra grew massive (Mahler, Wagner)
- Virtuosity became a spectacle (Liszt, Paganini)

**Key figures:** Frédéric Chopin (the poet of the piano), Richard Wagner (opera as total art), Pyotr Tchaikovsky (emotional intensity), Johannes Brahms (Romantic classicist).

**The guitar:** Paradoxically, the guitar fell behind. It could not compete with the volume of Romantic orchestras and pianos. Francisco Tárrega kept the flame alive, arranging orchestral works for guitar and developing modern technique. His work set the stage for the guitar's 20th-century revival.

---

## 7. Modern Period (c. 1900–present)

**What it sounded like:** Everything. Atonality, jazz, minimalism, electronic music, world fusion. The 20th century shattered the rules and rebuilt them in a hundred different ways.

**Key developments:**
- Atonality and twelve-tone technique (Schoenberg) — abandoning the key system entirely
- Jazz (1900s onward) — improvisation, swing, blues harmony
- Minimalism (Reich, Glass, Riley) — repetition and gradual change
- Electronic music — synthesizers, computers, entirely new sounds
- Rock, pop, and the electric guitar — music became global mass culture
- World music fusion — traditions that had been separate for centuries began to blend

**Key figures:** Igor Stravinsky (revolutionary orchestration), Arnold Schoenberg (atonality), Miles Davis (jazz evolution), The Beatles (popular music as art).

**The guitar:** Andrés Segovia made the classical guitar a concert instrument. The electric guitar (1930s onward) changed everything — blues, rock, jazz, metal. The guitar became the most popular instrument in the world.

---

## 8. The Big Picture — How Harmony Evolved

| Period | Harmony | Texture |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Medieval | Monophonic → early parallel motion | Single line → 2-3 voices |
| Renaissance | Full polyphony, consonance rules | 4-6 independent voices |
| Baroque | Tonal harmony, basso continuo | Melody + bass + fill |
| Classical | Clear chord progressions, functional harmony | Melody + accompaniment |
| Romantic | Chromaticism, extended chords | Rich orchestral layers |
| Modern | Atonality, jazz harmony, modal, free | Anything goes |

The pattern: each period pushed harmony further, until the Romantic period stretched tonality to its breaking point, and the Modern period either broke it entirely or found new ways to use it.

---

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Polyphony | Multiple independent melodic lines sounding simultaneously |
| Tonality | Music organized around a central key (major or minor) |
| Chromaticism | Using notes outside the current key for color or tension |
| Counterpoint | The art of combining independent melodies according to rules |
| Basso continuo | A continuous bass line that provides harmonic foundation (Baroque) |
| Sonata form | A musical structure: exposition, development, recapitulation |

## Self-Check Assessment

**1. Which period introduced the major/minor key system we still use in pop music?**
> The Baroque period (c. 1600–1750) established tonal harmony.

**2. Why did the guitar decline during the Romantic period?**
> It could not match the volume of the growing Romantic orchestra and piano. It was too quiet for large concert halls.

**3. Name one composer from each period.**
> Medieval: Hildegard of Bingen. Renaissance: Josquin des Prez. Baroque: Bach. Classical: Mozart. Romantic: Chopin. Modern: Stravinsky. (Many correct answers exist.)

**4. What was the guitar's ancestor, and how did it reach Europe?**
> The oud, which arrived in Europe via Moorish Spain.

**Pass criteria:** Can name all six periods in order, identify at least one harmonic development per period, and explain the guitar's evolution from oud to electric.
